## What the stats strip shows

On your athlete's profile page, you'll see a row of key numbers just below their name and position. These are called the season stats strip. Each number is meant to give you a quick snapshot of how your child is developing — not a grade, and not a judgment. Think of them as growth markers you can track week to week across the season.

## Games played

This is simply the number of games your athlete was active and participated in this season. It does not count games they attended but sat out due to injury or other reasons.

## Average grade

The average grade is a score from 1 to 10 that reflects your athlete's consistency and execution over the season. Coaches assign a grade to each game based on how well your child carried out their assignments and improved on areas of focus. A 7 or 8 is solid. A 10 is exceptional. If the number is lower early in the season, that's normal — it takes time to build consistency, and the number is designed to trend upward as your athlete grows.

## Position-specific stats

Depending on where your child plays, you'll see stats that make sense for their role.

**Running backs and receivers** — You'll see carries (how many times a runner got the ball handed to them), targets (how many times a receiver was thrown to), and yards gained. These numbers show how involved your athlete was in the offense and how they performed when given the opportunity.

**Linemen** — You'll see a block grade, expressed as a percentage. This represents the share of blocking assignments your child successfully completed. An 80% block grade means they executed their assignment 8 out of 10 times — a strong result at any level.

**Defenders** — You'll see tackles (how many times your athlete made or assisted on a stop) and turnovers (fumble recoveries or interceptions credited to them). These numbers reward effort and awareness, not just athleticism.

## Snap percentage

Snap percentage tells you how much of the game your athlete was on the field. A 100% snap percentage means they played every play. Anything lower may reflect a rotation, a position group with multiple players, or a specific game plan — it is not necessarily a reflection of performance. If you have questions about playing time, that's always a great conversation to have directly with your coach.

## A note on how to read these stats

These numbers are developmental metrics. They are meant to help you and your athlete see progress over time — not to rank kids against each other or define who they are as players. A child who improves their block grade from 60% to 80% over a season is making real progress, even if that number doesn't appear on a highlight reel. The goal is growth, and these stats are one way to see it.
